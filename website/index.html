General Information

Nutrition

Aim for 2 servings of fruit and 5 of veg a day. A variety keeps your gut and energy levels happy.

Hydration

Keep a water bottle with you. Even mild dehydration can cause fatigue and headaches.

Sleep

Stick to a regular routine; 7–9 hours helps memory, mood, and focus.

Mental health

Take 10 minutes daily for something calming (deep breathing, journaling, stretching).

Topics

Burns

Burns damage the skin and need quick, proper care.

Cool the burn under running water for 20 minutes. Remove tight items, cover with a sterile, non-stick dressing, and seek help for severe or large burns.

Bleeding

Blood loss that needs firm pressure to control.

Apply direct pressure with a clean cloth, elevate if possible, and keep pressure until bleeding stops. Call emergency help for severe bleeding.

Choking

Airway blocked—needs rapid action.

Encourage coughing. If ineffective, give back blows and chest/abdominal thrusts per guidelines. Call emergency services if obstruction persists.

CPR

Keeps blood and oxygen flowing.

Call emergency services. Push hard and fast in the centre of the chest (about 100–120/min) and use an AED as soon as available.

Cuts

Skin breaks needing clean & cover.

Rinse with clean water, pat dry, apply antiseptic if available, and cover with a sterile dressing. Watch for signs of infection.

Fracture

Broken bone—support & seek care.

Immobilise the area as found, apply a splint if trained, and seek medical care. Do not push bones back in.

Poison

Harmful substances—urgent help.

Do not induce vomiting. Check the label if available and call Poison Information (Australia: 13 11 26) or emergency services.

Shock

Body not getting enough blood flow.

Lay the person down, keep warm, elevate legs if no injury, and get urgent medical help.

Stings

Venom can cause pain & swelling.

A bee sting injects venom, causing redness, swelling, and pain. Remove the stinger quickly, clean the area, and apply a cold pack.

First Aid Do’s and Don’ts

Do’s

  • Move someone with a suspected head, neck, or back injury.

  • Call 000 (or your local emergency number) if needed.

  • Stay calm and assess the situation before acting.

Don’ts

  • Move someone with a suspected head, neck, or back injury.

  • Give food or drink to an unconscious or seriously injured person.

  • Panic or rush in without checking for danger.

Dr's ABCD

Danger Response Send Airway Breathing CPR Defibrillation
D = Danger

In first aid, DRSABCD starts with D for Danger. Before providing help, always check for any risks to yourself, the injured person, and bystanders. Ensuring the area is safe prevents further harm and lets you give first aid without putting yourself in danger.

Check for Danger
  • Look around the scene before approaching.
  • Identify hazards — fire, traffic, sharp objects, electricity.
  • Check for danger to you, the casualty, then others.
  • Never rush in — your safety comes first.
Common Hazards
  • Traffic – cars, bikes, moving vehicles
  • Fire/Smoke – flames, heat, fumes
  • Electricity – wires, power lines, equipment
  • Chemicals or gas leaks
  • Broken glass, unstable structures
  • Aggressive people or crowds
Make the Area Safe
  • Turn off power sources / move away from danger.
  • Keep bystanders at a safe distance.
  • Use barriers like gloves or masks when possible.
  • Secure hazards before approaching the injured person.
Tips and Techniques
  • Stop and scan first — don’t act until it’s safe.
  • Never risk your own life to save someone else.
  • Call for help early if danger can’t be controlled.
  • Stay alert for new risks while giving first aid.
R = Response

In first aid, the second step of DRSABCD is R for Response. Once the area is safe, check if the injured person is conscious and able to respond. This helps you understand their condition and decide what to do next.

How to Check for Response
  • Talk — “Can you hear me?” “Open your eyes.”
  • Touch — Gently tap shoulders or squeeze hands.
  • Observe — Movement, blinking, facial changes.
  • Listen — Moaning or breathing sounds.
If They Respond
  • Leave in a comfortable position.
  • Check for injuries or bleeding.
  • Monitor until help arrives.
  • Reassure and keep warm.
If They Don’t Respond
  • Shout for help / call emergency services.
  • Proceed to S = Send for Help.
Tips and Techniques
  • Introduce yourself before touching.
  • Stay calm and speak clearly.
  • Get help early if unresponsive.
  • No response = emergency.
S = Send for Help

After checking for danger and a response, the next step in DRSABCD is S for Send for Help. Early medical assistance saves lives.

When to Call for Help
  • Unresponsive, not breathing, or seriously injured.
  • Any life-threatening emergency (severe bleeding, burns, poisoning).
  • When more than basic first aid is needed.
How to Send for Help
  • Call 000 — ask for Ambulance.
  • Give location, what happened, number of people hurt.
  • Use speakerphone; send a bystander if present.
  • Remote area: satellite phone / PLB if available.
  • Don’t hang up until told to.
Give Clear Information
  • Provide your exact location — street name, landmarks, or building number.
  • Describe what happened (e.g. injury, burn, allergic reaction, collapse).
  • State how many people are injured or need help.
  • Explain the person’s condition — are they conscious, breathing, or bleeding?
  • Stay calm and speak clearly so the operator can assist quickly.
Tips and Techniques
  • Keep your phone on loudspeaker so both hands stay free.
  • Ask someone nearby to call for help if you’re busy assisting the injured person.
  • Stay calm and speak slowly — the operator will guide you step by step.
  • Don’t hang up until told to — emergency operators can provide CPR instructions.
  • Send someone to meet and guide the ambulance to your location.
A = Airway

Ensure the person’s airway is clear and open. A blocked airway prevents oxygen reaching the lungs.

Check the Airway
  • Lay on back on a firm surface.
  • Open mouth; look for vomit, food, blood.
  • Roll to side to drain fluids if needed.
  • Remove visible obstructions with fingers/cloth.
  • No blind finger sweeps.
Clear the Airway
  • Only remove what you can see.
  • Check under the tongue (dentures, vomit).
  • Return to back and recheck for breathing.
Keep it Open
  • Head Tilt–Chin Lift.
  • Suspected spine injury: Jaw Thrust.
  • Maintain while checking breathing.
Tips & Next Steps
  • Check for normal breathing ≤10 seconds.
  • Breathing: place in Recovery Position.
  • Not breathing: start CPR.
B = Breathing

Decide if the person is breathing normally and whether CPR is needed.

Check for Breathing
  • Keep airway open (Head Tilt–Chin Lift).
  • Look, listen, feel for ≤10 seconds.
Normal Signs
  • Regular chest rise and fall.
  • Normal breath sounds; air felt at mouth/nose.
  • Normal skin colour.
If Not Normal
  • Confirm 000 is called.
  • Start CPR (30:2).
  • Use a barrier if available.
Tips
  • Agonal gasps ≠ normal breathing.
  • Recheck after ~2 mins of CPR if unsure.
  • If breathing resumes: recovery position + monitor.
C = CPR

CPR keeps blood and oxygen flowing until help arrives.

Starting CPR
  • Firm, flat surface; kneel beside chest.
  • Hands centre chest (lower half sternum).
  • Arms straight; shoulders over hands.
Compressions
  • Depth ≈ one-third chest (~5 cm adults).
  • Rate 100–120/min (“Stayin’ Alive”).
  • Full recoil each push; 30 compressions.
Rescue Breaths
  • Open airway; pinch nose; seal mouth.
  • Give 2 breaths — just to see chest rise.
  • Hands-only CPR if you can’t give breaths.
Tips & Safety
  • Alternate 30:2; swap rescuer every ~2 mins.
  • Don’t stop until breathing returns, help arrives, or you’re exhausted.
  • Use an AED ASAP; follow operator prompts.
D = Defibrillation

Use an AED to restore a normal heart rhythm during cardiac arrest. Early defibrillation greatly improves survival.

What is an AED?
  • Portable device that analyses rhythm, delivers shocks if needed.
  • Found in schools, malls, gyms, airports, offices.
  • Gives clear voice/visual instructions.
How to Use
  • Turn on; follow prompts.
  • Expose/dry chest; remove metal/jewellery in pad area.
  • Place pads: upper right chest & lower left side.
  • Stand clear during analysis/shock.
  • Resume CPR immediately after shock or “no shock.”
While Using
  • Continue CPR between analyses.
  • Keep pads on for monitoring.
  • Avoid water/metal surfaces if possible.
  • Assign tasks if multiple rescuers.
Key Reminders
  • Time is critical — use AED ASAP.
  • Trust the prompts; stay calm.
  • Tell paramedics usage time and number of shocks.

Inside a First Aid Kit

Gloves

Gloves are an essential item in any first aid kit because they protect both the first aider and the injured person from infection or contamination. Wearing disposable gloves helps prevent contact with blood, body fluids, or other potentially harmful substances, reducing the risk of spreading germs or disease. They also help keep wounds clean while providing a safe barrier when giving care.
Scissors

Medical scissors are an important part of a first aid kit because they allow you to quickly and safely cut clothing, bandages, or tape during an emergency. Their blunt-ended design helps prevent accidental injury while cutting near the skin. Having medical scissors ensures you can access a wound easily, apply dressings properly, and remove materials without causing further harm.
BandAids

Band-aids (adhesive dressings) are a key item in any first aid kit because they protect small cuts, blisters, or abrasions from dirt, bacteria, and further injury. They help the wound stay clean and promote faster healing by keeping it covered and moist. Band-aids also provide a small cushion to reduce pain or friction on the affected area.
Pain Killers

Painkillers are included in a first aid kit to help relieve minor pain, headaches, muscle aches, or injuries until further treatment is available. Common options like paracetamol or ibuprofen can reduce discomfort, inflammation, and fever. Having painkillers on hand allows for quick relief in emergency or everyday situations, helping the injured person stay calm and comfortable.
Cold Packs

Cold packs are an essential first aid item used to reduce swelling, pain, and inflammation from injuries such as sprains, bruises, or insect bites. When activated, they provide instant cooling that helps constrict blood vessels and minimize tissue damage. Cold packs are especially useful for treating soft tissue injuries and providing comfort until further medical care is available.
Cotton Buds

Cotton buds are a useful addition to a first aid kit because they allow for precise cleaning and application of ointments or antiseptics to small wounds or delicate areas. Their small tips make it easier to treat cuts, scrapes, or insect bites without contaminating the rest of the dressing. They help maintain hygiene and accuracy when applying first aid care.
Tweezers

Tweezers are an important tool in a first aid kit because they help remove small foreign objects such as splinters, glass, or debris from the skin safely. Using tweezers prevents further injury or infection by allowing precise removal without direct contact. They’re also useful for handling sterile dressings or removing ticks during outdoor first aid situations.
Alcoholic Wipes

Alcoholic wipes are included in a first aid kit to clean and disinfect the skin or small wounds before applying a dressing or bandage. They help kill bacteria and reduce the risk of infection. Alcohol wipes can also be used to sanitize hands or medical tools, ensuring that first aid is given in a clean and safe manner.
